<div class="sec">
  <div class="eyebrow">Abstract</div>
  <div class="abstract">
    <p class="lead">Traffic safety is usually treated as a snapshot. This paper shows it is a <b>process</b> — risk builds up and recovers along different paths during a disturbance, tracing a loop.</p>
    <p>Using real-world trajectory datasets, this study demonstrates that safety hysteresis exists at both the vehicle-pair and platoon levels, manifested as counterclockwise loops on spacing–TTC<sup>−1</sup> diagrams and clockwise loops on density–TTC<sup>−1</sup> diagrams. To quantify these patterns, we propose the Safety Hysteresis Intensity (HI), defined as the normalized area enclosed by the safety hysteresis loop. Empirical analyses show that naturalistic traffic exhibits substantially higher safety HI than controlled experiments, while ACC and autonomous vehicles reduce safety HI and promote safer conditions. Correlation analyses reveal that low-speed regimes, large inter-vehicle speed differences, and abrupt acceleration or deceleration amplify pair-level safety HI, whereas variability in flow, density, and average speed governs platoon-level safety HI. Complementary simulations further clarify these mechanisms, showing that higher braking intensity and greater macroscopic fluctuations lead to stronger safety hysteresis.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="sec">
  <div class="eyebrow">The idea</div>
  <h2>Safety has a memory</h2>
  <p>Instantaneous measures such as Time-to-Collision capture risk at a single moment, but not the <b>path</b> safety takes through a disturbance. At the same spacing or density, the safety state during risk build-up differs from the state during recovery — the signature of hysteresis. The safety axis is the inverse Time-to-Collision, where a higher value means more hazardous: $$TTC^{-1}_n=\dfrac{\Delta v_n}{s_n}\ \text{ if } v_n>v_{n-1},\ \text{ else } 0.$$</p>

  </div>
  <div class="defs">
    <div class="def">
      <div class="k">HI — vehicle pair</div>
      $$I_s=\dfrac{|A|}{\Delta s_A}$$
      <div class="d">Loop area on the spacing–TTC<sup>−1</sup> plane, divided by the spacing span.</div>
    </div>
    <div class="def">
      <div class="k">HI — platoon</div>
      $$\mathbb{I}_s=\dfrac{|\mathbb{A}|}{\Delta k_{\mathbb{A}}}$$
      <div class="d">Loop area on the density–TTC<sup>−1</sup> plane, divided by the density span.</div>
    </div>
  </div>
  <p>A larger HI means more severe, volatile safety conditions; a smaller HI means smoother, safer transitions. Three datasets are analysed: <b>NGSIM</b> (naturalistic human driving), <b>OpenACC</b> (controlled ACC experiments), and <b>Waymo</b> (real-world autonomous driving).</p>
</div>

<div class="sec">
  <div class="eyebrow">A physical constraint</div>
  <h2>The pair-level loop is always counterclockwise</h2>
  <p>Unlike traditional traffic hysteresis — whose loop direction depends on whether a driver is timid or aggressive — safety hysteresis at the vehicle-pair level is <b>always counterclockwise</b>. It is a physical necessity: a loop can only begin when the follower is faster than the leader ($$v_F>v_L$$), and that same condition forces the counterclockwise direction.</p>
